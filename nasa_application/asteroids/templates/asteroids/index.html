NEAR-EARTH OBJECTS

Potentially Hazardous Asteroids (PHAs)

Potentially Hazardous Asteroids, or PHAs, are celestial bodies that orbit close to Earth and possess characteristics that could pose a significant threat to our planet.

Defined by their size, trajectory, and proximity, PHAs are monitored closely by astronomers and space agencies worldwide as part of global planetary defense initiatives.

PHAs are classified based on two key criteria: their minimum orbit intersection distance (MOID) with Earth, which must be 0.05 astronomical units (AU) or less, and their size, typically exceeding 140 meters in diameter.

NASA Database

The database used in this project is sourced directly from NASA’s extensive collection of near-Earth object (NEO) data, containing over 90,000 records of asteroids and their characteristics. For our analysis, we utilized a curated subset of 17,000 entries, carefully balanced to ensure an accurate and unbiased classification process.

The dataset includes crucial attributes such as maximum and minimum diameter, relative velocity, miss distance, and absolute magnitude. These features provide key insights into the potential hazards posed by asteroids and their trajectories near Earth. Leveraging this rich and detailed data, we aim to enhance understanding and prediction of potentially hazardous asteroids (PHAs).

This comprehensive approach highlights the importance of NASA’s efforts in making data publicly available, empowering researchers worldwide to contribute to planetary defense and space exploration initiatives.

Artificial Intelligence

As NASA explores the mysteries of the cosmos, advancements in artificial intelligence play a vital role in analyzing and interpreting vast amounts of data. In this project, we utilized a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), a type of artificial neural network, to assess and classify asteroid data. This approach allows for high-precision predictions, aiding in the identification of potentially hazardous asteroids (PHAs).

The MLP model processes multiple features, such as asteroid diameter, relative velocity, miss distance, and absolute magnitude, to uncover patterns that might not be immediately visible through traditional methods. Its ability to learn complex relationships in the data ensures a robust and reliable analysis, contributing to planetary defense initiatives and our understanding of near-Earth objects.

By integrating AI technologies like the MLP, we not only enhance our predictive capabilities but also pave the way for smarter and more efficient methods to process the wealth of data generated by NASA's missions. The future of space exploration and planetary protection relies on the seamless collaboration between human ingenuity and artificial intelligence.

A History of Innovation

For decades, NASA has led the charge in understanding and mitigating the risks posed by near-Earth objects (NEOs), including potentially hazardous asteroids (PHAs). With a commitment to planetary defense, NASA has developed sophisticated technologies and strategies to monitor and study these celestial objects, ensuring Earth's safety while advancing our knowledge of the solar system.

Programs like the Planetary Defense Coordination Office (PDCO) and missions such as DART (Double Asteroid Redirection Test) exemplify NASA's proactive approach to addressing asteroid impact risks. By leveraging state-of-the-art telescopes, satellite observations, and global collaborations, NASA continually refines its ability to detect and track PHAs with remarkable accuracy.

This dedication not only safeguards our planet but also highlights NASA’s ongoing legacy of innovation, turning potential threats into opportunities for scientific discovery and technological advancement.

Asteroid Apophis: Dangerous for Earth?

The asteroid Apophis, discovered in 2004, has captured the attention of scientists and space enthusiasts due to its size and unique trajectory.

With an estimated diameter of approximately 370 meters, Apophis is one of the largest asteroids to pass close to Earth. Apophis has been closely monitored by various space agencies, including NASA, which uses advanced telescopes and radar technologies to study its orbit and behavior. NASA is also exploring ways to deflect asteroids on a collision course with Earth, a scenario that, while unlikely, could have serious consequences for life on our planet.

During its closest approach, scheduled for 2029, Apophis will be just 31,000 kilometers from Earth a distance considered extremely close compared to the orbit of geosynchronous satellites.

This event has generated significant concern, though the chances of an impact remain extremely low. However, the study of Apophis remains a priority for space research, providing critical insights into asteroid dynamics and the potential threats they pose.

Asteroid Monitoring Missions

Discover how several space missions are monitoring near-Earth asteroids and exploring methods of defending against impacts.

NEOWISE

NEOWISE is a NASA satellite focused on detecting near-Earth asteroids and comets. The mission was relaunched in 2013 and is vital for monitoring dangerous objects.

OSIRIS-REx

The OSIRIS-REx mission collects samples from the asteroid Bennu for study. Launched in 2016, it also provides crucial data for understanding impact risks.

AIDA (DART)

The AIDA mission, including the DART mission, aims to test asteroid deflection methods to protect Earth from possible collisions.

Hayabusa2

The Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) Hayabusa2 mission was launched to collect samples from the asteroid Ryugu.
